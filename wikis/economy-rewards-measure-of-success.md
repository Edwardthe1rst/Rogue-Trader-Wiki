## Experience Points

The most common [Rewards](economy-rewards.md) are experience points (xp). More than anything else, xp measures a character's personal power. Players use experience points to buy new Skills, Talents, and Advances to improve their characters. The more xp a character possesses, the better he will be at dealing with challenges in play, such as overcoming foes in [Combat](rules-combat-overview.md), solving problems, or dominating social encounters. Because xp relate directly to a character's power, how much and how often the GM awards experience points to his players will determine how quickly their characters rise to power and how able they will be at defeating any challenges he should throw at them.

There are two main ways for the GM to award experience to his players: the Abstract Method and [The Detailed Method](mass-combats.md#the-detailed-method).

### Abstract Method

This is the easiest and recommended (certainly for beginning GMs) way to award experience. It relies simply on the amount of time spent gaming and ensures a steady and smooth rise in power for the characters. For each game session each player should receive 500 xp.

This method assumes a game session lasts about 4 hours. For  longer  or  shorter  sessions,  the  GM  can  adjust  the  xp [Rewards](economy-rewards.md) accordingly.

### Detailed Method

A  more  detailed  method  of  awarding  experience  is  also possible, in which each reward is broken down on a case-bycase basis, and players gain experience for completing specific tasks. For this method to work, however, the GM must assign a  value  to  each  encounter.  For  [Example](rules-tests.md),  he  might  have  a session that includes a dangerous journey through [The Warp](warp-imperial-space-travel.md), a space battle against orks, and a tense negotiation with some hostile natives. In this case, each of the three encounters would be worth a set amount of xp that would be awarded to the players when the encounters were completed or overcome.

Table  10-1:  Encounter  Difficulty gives  examples  of encounters  based  on  difficulty.  To  work  out  the  difficulty of  an  encounter,  consider  how  much  it  taxes  the  resources f  the  group and what it costs them in [Wounds](character-injury.md), equipment, or time.

When  using  this  method,  it  is  important  to  remember to  award  experience  for  only  meaningful  encounters  and obstacles, lest the players embark on the wholesale slaughter of worlds simply for the 'experience.' Likewise, as the player characters gain in power, some challengers will be so easily overcome as to be worth little or no experience.

### Roleplaying Awards

The  GM  may  also  offer  experience  for  good  roleplaying. These awards can be as large or small as the GM feels is fair, typically anywhere between 100xp and 500xp, and are given to players that the GM felt did a good job in portraying their characters. The GM should be careful however only to give out these awards when he feels they are really deserving and usually  only  when  the  player  does  something  beyond  the norm, such as taking an action that he knows may place his character in danger because it is 'what his character would do.'## [Profit Factor](economy-wealth-and-acquisitions.md) [Rewards](economy-rewards.md)

In  the  same  manner  as  Experience  [Rewards](economy-rewards.md),  the  GM  may decide  that  the  individual  actions  of  the  Explorers  merit  a [Profit Factor](economy-wealth-and-acquisitions.md) award. Typically, however, all gains by the PCs to Profit Factor will be as a result of [Endeavours](economy-endeavours.md) (see page 276) as such vast sums of wealth or increases in [Influence](economy-influence-rules.md) and power take time and planning to amass. The option is available if the GM decides that either as the result of the plot or through outstanding  actions  of  the  Explorers  they  warrant  such  an award.  As  a  guideline,  Profit  Factor  awards  should  be  used sparingly and only ever increase Profit Factor by 1 point.

## Game Rewards

In addition to awarding experience and Profit, the GM can also award in-game things such as [Influence](economy-influence-rules.md), power, and rank. Typically  these  are  things  earned  through  the  completing of specific quests or missions set out by powerful NPCs or through actions that alter the very fabric of the game world. Because these kinds of [Rewards](economy-rewards.md) are more difficult to adjudicate it is up to the GM to decide when and how they are used. They can, however, be more valuable that any of the other kinds of [Rewards](economy-rewards.md), giving the characters things that skill with a bolter or a bigger ship could not achieve.

## Awarding Fate Points

Finally, it is also possible that the Game Master may reward players  with  a  permanent  Fate  Point  for  truly  remarkable deeds. The GM should use such [Rewards](economy-rewards.md) sparingly and only award them for something incredible.

## When to Use Rewards

Most [Rewards](economy-rewards.md) are awarded at the end of a game session, when the GM can tally up the PCs' achievements for that session. The GM may wish to wait until the start of the next game session to hand out [Rewards](economy-rewards.md), giving himself the time between games to work out what the PCs have accomplished. Some rewards,  however,  like  Profit  and  game  rewards,  might  be handed out during play as they are earned. Profit and other such  benefits  will  fluctuate  greatly  during  the  course  of  a Rogue Trader's adventures and awarding these as they crop up in play can make progress easier to track.
